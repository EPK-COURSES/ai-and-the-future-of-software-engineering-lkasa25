# Future of Software Engineering Jobs



## Introduction



Software Engineering has changed drastically with the development of Artificial Intelligence. Nowadays the entire concept of a software developer has greatly shifted. Nowadays writing code manually is no longer required from a developer, instead AI is used as an assisting tool which can generate code, debug and automate various development tasks. Due to this, a software engineer's responsibilities are beginning to change and so is our work.

AI-assisted tools are being used by various companies to help cut down on development time and also increase productivity. However, this does not translate to software engineers becoming irrelevant in the field. Rather, the usage of AI has changed the skills and capabilities that a software developer needs to have in order to be in the job market.

During this assignment I will investigate the impact of AI on software engineering jobs, focusing on junior developers, expectations from seniors and the significance of human skills which are hard to automate.


## The Impact of AI in Junior Engineer Roles 

In the past, the traditional journey of a junior developer started with simple tasks such as fixing bugs, writing basic functions and documenting. These tasks helped develop problem-solving skills and building muscle memory for syntax. However with the rise of AI tools such as Claude, ChatGPT or GitHub Copilot these manual simple tasks are transitioning towards automated execution. According to  research by Abu Zohair (2019) on the path of software engineering towards the 2050s with AI changing the fundamental software development lifecycle phases. 
Previously developers spent most of their time coding and bug-fixing while now AI tools greatly reduce the time needed to complete this tasks. Zohair also highlights that instead of replacing programmers, this shift makes junior engineers adapt new technical skills immediately. 
The study's qualitative survey indicates that software engineers remain the "main actors who shape the future." For junior engineers, this means the barrier to entry is no longer just memorizing syntax or writing  code, instead, their roles are transforming to focus heavily on AI oversight, prompt engineering, and verifying the logic and security of auto-generated snippets.


## Future Expectations from Senior Engineers

As AI systems absorb routine code execution, the professional focus of senior software engineers is shifting heavily toward systemic oversight, technical leadership, and business alignment. In his industry-standard text, "The Software Engineer's Guidebook", Gergely Orosz (2024) establishes that true seniority in software engineering is never measured solely by lines of code written, but by an engineer's ability to manage ambiguity, design scalable architectures, and multiply the impact of their team.
The rise of generative AI tools amplifies Orosz’s framework. When code generation becomes cheap and immediate, the primary expectation for a senior engineer transforms from a "builder" into a "systems thinker" and "guardrail designer." According to Orosz, senior developers are uniquely responsible for handling technical complexity and mitigating structural risks. Because automated AI models lack global contextual awareness and frequently introduce architectural drift or  security defects, senior engineers must act as rigorous system stewards. Their core value shifts toward defining strict architectural boundaries, implementing robust test-driven development (TDD) environments, and ensuring that auto-generated modules securely integrate into large-scale, multi-layered company ecosystems.
Furthermore, Orosz emphasizes that an expectation of senior engineering is team leverage through communication and mentorship. In an AI-accelerated environment, senior engineers are expected to pivot away from manual programming and scale their influence by mentoring early-career developers. They must teach junior engineers how to safely validate AI logic, properly break down massive business objectives into modular system designs, and align technical execution with overarching business outcomes—areas of cognitive judgment and human leadership that automated agents cannot emulate.
